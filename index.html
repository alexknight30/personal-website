Cookie Monster's Favorite Chocolate Chip Recipe

Wet Ingredients

  • Unsalted butter – 240g (16% water)
  • Granulated sugar – 190g (0% water)
  • Brown sugar — 170g (2% water)
  • Eggs – 2 × 52 g = 104g (74% water)
  • Pure vanilla extract – 12g (35% water)

Dry Ingredients

  • All-purpose flour – 300g
  • Baking soda – 5g
  • Fine sea salt – 4g
  • Chocolate chips – 360g
  • Mystery aromatic ingredient – 14g

Directions

  1. Cream the butter with both sugars until light and fluffy
  2. Beat in eggs and vanilla until smooth
  3. In a separate bowl, whisk together flour, baking soda, salt, and mystery ingredient
  4. Fold dry into wet, then stir in chocolate chips
  5. Scoop dough onto trays, noting that 3 of every 5 cookies are smaller (90% of average weight) and 2 of every 5 are larger (115% of average). The average cookie weighs 26.66g!
  6. Bake at 175 °C (347–350 °F), center rack. On dark pans reduce temp by 5 °C; on convection, reduce by 10–12 °C and check 2 min earlier
  7. Cool on sheet 2 minutes, then rack

Bakers Notes

Hmmmm cookie. Not so fast! The oven is running a little hot, so all of the water content is lost to the heat. After this, the Maillard reaction claims another 1.8% of that total. Lastly, Cookie Monster gets confused easily, so it's best to keep everything in the same units! How many cookies will Cookie Monster have????

Goal: Find the optimal network configuration - too few neurons and the image will be blurry, too many and you'll overfit!

When Death Comes

When death comes
like the hungry bear in autumn;
when death comes and takes all the bright coins from his purse

to buy me, and snaps the purse shut;
when death comes
like the measle-pox;

when death comes
like an iceberg between the shoulder blades,

I want to step through the door full of curiosity, wondering:
what is it going to be like, that cottage of darkness?

And therefore I look upon everything
as a brotherhood and a sisterhood,
and I look upon time as no more than an idea,
and I consider eternity as another possibility,

and I think of each life as a flower, as common
as a field daisy, and as singular,

and each name a comfortable music in the mouth,
tending, as all music does, toward silence,

and each body a lion of courage, and something
precious to the earth.

When it's over, I want to say: all my life
I was a bride married to amazement.
I was the bridegroom, taking the world into my arms.

When it's over, I don't want to wonder
if I have made of my life something particular, and real.
I don't want to find myself sighing and frightened,
or full of argument.

I don't want to end up simply having visited this world.

—Mary Oliver
